Here's some delicacies you should try!

From sweet and flaky egg tarts to seafood-studded soupy rice, Portugal's exquisite cuisine is available in Michelin-starred restaurants, quaint taverns, and sidewalk charcoal grills. Don't pass up these culinary apex of Portugal. Apetite bon!

1. Bifanas

Thin slices of pork are marinated and cooked in a sauce mixture of white wine, garlic, and paprika to make bifanas, which are traditionally eaten in Portugal on soft rolls with plenty of mustard and piri-piri sauce. According to history, the original one originates in the southern Portuguese town of Vendas Novas in the Alentejo region. If you visit,be sure to sample the ones at Café Boavista. The searing steak with mustard makes it feel out of body, and the bread is toasted and crunchy.

You know every place will say THEY have the original, but let’s not waste time discussing which one is “the original”. Instead, let’s say “Who cares which one is the original! I just want the best one!”

2. The Francesinha

The northern region of Portugal, notably the city of Porto, is where the Portuguese Francesinha sandwich originates. Don't think of francesinhas as a simple, light sandwich that you consume when you have some empty space in your stomach. This dish weighs A LOT. In other words, eat up it when you are famished and have sufficient room for this monstrosity.

A rich, meaty sauce, bread, sausages, beef, ham, cheese, and egg. All of this is served with a lot of french fries. That is what I consider a full meal!

3. Pastel de Nata

Portuguese meaning "cream pastries," "pasteteis de nata" characterises the velvety smoothness of this traditional meal. In a monastery west of Lisbon, the pastel de nata was first created more than 300 years ago. After utilising egg whites for laundry, monks created a custard-filled dessert recipe with sugar and extra yolks from eggs. A tempting taste of history is the crisp golden pastry covered with a smooth, velvety cream.

If you're looking for desserts after having your meal in Portugal, having Pastel de Nata would be the perfect choice!

5. Ginja (ginjinha)

In Lisbon, a glass of ginja only costs one or two euros, making it particularly difficult to resist. It is simple to understand why everyone, from Lisbon locals to foreign tourists, is smitten with this liqueur. It is extremely cheap, dangerously tasty, and 100 percent authentic. Once you give it a try, you might want to join them.
